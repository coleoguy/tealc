## Heath Blackmon, Texas A&M University

---

Chromosomes are the physical scaffolding of inheritance, yet the forces that govern their number, structure, and sex-determining function remain poorly resolved. Chromosome number varies more than 1,000-fold across eukaryotes; sex chromosomes arise, differentiate, and are lost repeatedly across the tree of life; and the epistatic interactions among loci distributed across chromosomes shape the phenotypic response to selection in ways that classical quantitative genetics cannot fully capture. My research program integrates theory, comparative genomics, and large-scale databases to understand why genomes are structured the way they are — and why that structure changes.

The original MIRA award (R35GM138098, 2020–2025) supported three interconnected lines of work: (1) estimating rates and drivers of chromosome number evolution across animals using the largest karyotype databases ever assembled; (2) developing and testing the Fragile Y hypothesis — that recurrent selection to reduce X-Y recombination creates a small pseudoautosomal region that, in chiasmatic species, elevates Y-chromosome aneuploidy and drives Y loss; and (3) building theory and empirical tests for how epistasis shapes the response to selection and the maintenance of genetic variance. The award period produced 18 peer-reviewed publications, six open karyotype databases (>63,000 records spanning Coleoptera, Diptera, Mammalia, Amphibia, Polyneoptera, and Drosophila), and two widely-used R packages (ChromePlus, evobiR). A preprint now in review at a top-tier journal reports 844-fold variation in dysploidy rates across 55 eukaryotic clades from 63,682 karyotypes — the most comprehensive analysis of chromosome number evolution yet conducted.

The renewal extends this program along three aims that build directly on these results while opening new empirical and theoretical territory.

---

**Aim 1. Determine the population-genetic and life-history predictors of chromosome number evolution across eukaryotes.**

Chromosome number evolves at rates spanning more than seven orders of magnitude. Our prior work in Coleoptera established that effective population size — proxied by range size and body size — predicts karyotype evolution rates better than any measure of selective pressure, consistent with the drift barrier hypothesis. The 63,682-karyotype dataset now enables this test across 55 eukaryotic clades simultaneously. We will fit phylogenetic models of dysploidy rate variation against a panel of predictors (Ne proxies, mating system, ploidy, sex chromosome system, generation time) using ChromoSSE and custom Bayesian frameworks. We will test whether the drift-dominated and selection-dominated regimes identified in beetles generalize across eukaryotes, and whether transitions in sex chromosome system predict shifts in karyotype evolution rate. **Expected outcome:** a unified quantitative model of what drives karyotype evolution rate variation across the tree of life, with direct implications for understanding genome instability in cancer and domestication.

**Aim 2. Test the mechanistic predictions of the Fragile Y hypothesis using comparative genomics in Coleoptera.**

The Fragile Y hypothesis predicts that (a) pseudoautosomal region (PAR) size is negatively correlated with Y-chromosome aneuploidy rate across chiasmatic species, (b) Y-chromosome loss is concentrated in lineages with small PARs, and (c) achiasmatic lineages are buffered against Y loss. Prior work established the comparative rate patterns; the renewal tests the molecular mechanism directly. We will estimate PAR size computationally from read-depth and allele-frequency data across 40 beetle species spanning the XY–Xy+–XO transition series, using a pipeline validated on the Cheirotonus formosanus genome assembly. We will test whether PAR size predicts Y-loss probability after controlling for phylogeny, and whether the PAR-size distribution differs between chiasmatic and achiasmatic clades as predicted. **Expected outcome:** the first direct empirical test of the PAR-size/aneuploidy mechanism across a comparative sample, converting the Fragile Y hypothesis from a rate-based inference to a mechanistically grounded model.

**Aim 3. Characterize the epistatic architecture of fitness-relevant traits and its consequences for adaptation.**

Epistasis — the non-additive interaction among loci — determines how genetic variance is partitioned, how populations respond to selection, and how quickly favorable combinations can be assembled or broken up by recombination. Our prior work showed that epistatic variance is routinely underestimated by standard quantitative-genetic designs and that the scale on which compound traits are measured fundamentally changes whether genetic effects appear additive or epistatic. The renewal pursues two extensions: (a) we will use line-cross analysis in Betta splendens and tomato to estimate the full epistatic architecture of domestication-related traits, testing whether domestication systematically depletes epistatic variance relative to additive variance; and (b) we will develop and validate a simulation framework (building on our existing ChromePlus infrastructure) that predicts the rate of adaptation as a function of chromosome number, epistasis level, and environmental fluctuation period — directly testing whether the modal karyotypes observed empirically (n=9 in Polyphaga; n=11/18 in Adephaga) correspond to fitness optima under realistic parameter ranges. **Expected outcome:** empirical estimates of epistatic architecture in two domesticated systems, and a theoretical framework connecting chromosome number to adaptive potential that generates testable predictions for any taxon with known karyotype and life-history data.

---

**Impact.** This program addresses a fundamental question in biology — why genomes are structured the way they are — using a combination of large-scale comparative data, mechanistic genomics, and quantitative theory that no other group is positioned to execute. The karyotype databases produced under this award are already the primary resource for comparative cytogenetics globally. The renewal will deliver the first unified model of karyotype evolution rate variation across eukaryotes, the first direct mechanistic test of the Fragile Y hypothesis, and a theoretical framework connecting genome architecture to adaptive potential. These outcomes will reshape how evolutionary biologists think about genome structure and provide a quantitative foundation for understanding karyotype instability in cancer, agriculture, and conservation.
