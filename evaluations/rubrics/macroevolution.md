# Rubric: Macroevolution

## What counts as on-topic

Content is on-topic if it addresses evolutionary patterns or processes operating at or above the species level across geological timescales. This includes diversification rates, trait-dependent speciation and extinction, adaptive radiations, convergent evolution, biogeography, mass extinctions, and the relationship between micro- and macroevolutionary processes. Purely population-genetic analyses with no connection to among-lineage patterns are off-topic.

## Standards for a testable hypothesis

A well-formed hypothesis specifies the clade or time window of interest, the macroevolutionary pattern predicted (e.g., elevated net diversification in lineages with trait X), the comparative method or model used to test it (e.g., BAMM, BiSSE, PGLS), and a clear null expectation. Hypotheses must acknowledge the known statistical power issues of diversification analyses at small taxonomic scales. Vague claims that a trait "promotes diversification" without a testable comparative framework do not meet the standard.

## What "grounding" means in this domain

Grounding means the output uses phylogenetic comparative methods appropriately and cites both the original method paper and a recent application to justify parameter choices. When invoking a diversification model, the output must acknowledge the assumptions of that model (e.g., constant-rate birth-death background for BiSSE). Paleontological claims must cite fossil record sources, not molecular phylogenies alone. Ancestral character reconstructions must note model choice and sensitivity to it.

## Red flags

- Treating the presence of many species in a clade as evidence of elevated speciation rate without a null model or stem-age correction
- Using sister-clade comparisons with grossly unequal clade ages without accounting for time
- Applying BiSSE or related models to clades with fewer than ~100 tips without discussing power limitations
- Conflating phenotypic convergence with shared ancestry in trait-diversification analyses
- Asserting a "key innovation" explanation without a test that distinguishes the innovation from correlated factors
- Ignoring extinction when making claims about net diversification from molecular phylogenies
