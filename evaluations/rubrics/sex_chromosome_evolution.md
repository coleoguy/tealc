# Rubric: Sex Chromosome Evolution

## What counts as on-topic

Content is on-topic if it addresses the origin, differentiation, or maintenance of sex chromosomes or sex-determination systems. This includes XY and ZW systems, transitions between systems (e.g., XY to XO, autosome-to-sex-chromosome turnover), dosage compensation, Y or W chromosome degeneration, and pseudoautosomal regions. Work on hermaphroditism or environmental sex determination is on-topic when framed in terms of the genomic machinery involved.

## Standards for a testable hypothesis

A well-formed hypothesis specifies which sex-determination system (or transition between systems) is under investigation, names a predicted genomic signature (e.g., elevated dN/dS on Y-linked genes, reduced heterozygosity on the non-recombining region), and links the prediction to a theoretical model (e.g., Hill-Robertson interference, mutation accumulation). Proposals that invoke "sex chromosome degeneration" without specifying a measurable proxy for degeneration are insufficiently precise.

## What "grounding" means in this domain

Grounding means the output correctly attributes predictions to their theoretical origin: Muller's ratchet, background selection, and selective sweeps each make distinct predictions and are not interchangeable explanations for Y degeneration. Citing Rice (1987) or Charlesworth et al. work correctly in context is grounded; citing them as generic support for "sex chromosomes evolve fast" is not. Empirical claims about a specific taxon must cite the primary molecular or cytogenetic study for that taxon.

## Red flags

- Treating all male-heterogametic systems (XY) as equivalent regardless of independent origins
- Assuming dosage compensation is universal or complete without citing evidence for the specific clade
- Conflating sex-limited inheritance with sex-biased gene expression
- Inferring Y degeneration solely from read-depth asymmetry without accounting for assembly or mapping artifacts
- Claiming a chromosome is "newly formed" based on sequence divergence alone without cytogenetic or phylogenetic context
