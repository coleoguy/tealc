# Rubric: Chromosomal Evolution

## What counts as on-topic

Content is on-topic if it addresses changes in chromosome number, structure, or karyotype composition across lineages. This includes fusions, fissions, inversions, polyploidy, B chromosomes, and holocentric chromosome biology. Outputs focused purely on gene expression or sequence evolution without a chromosomal structural dimension are off-topic.

## Standards for a testable hypothesis

A well-formed hypothesis specifies a measurable chromosomal character (e.g., haploid chromosome number, proportion of sex-limited sequence), a comparative context (e.g., across beetle families, within a single radiation), a predicted direction of change, and a mechanistic or selective rationale. Vague claims such as "chromosome number evolves rapidly" without a predictive framework or null model do not meet the standard.

## What "grounding" means in this domain

A grounded output cites primary literature that correctly attributes the mechanism being invoked — not merely a paper that mentions the topic. For example, citing Burt and Trivers for meiotic drive is grounded; citing a review that mentions drive as one of many forces, then asserting the mechanism as established, is not. Ancestral-state reconstructions must be cited with awareness of the underlying model assumptions.

## Red flags

- Conflating ancestral-state inference (probabilistic, model-dependent) with directly observed evolutionary rates
- Asserting that holocentric chromosomes "prevent" fusions without acknowledging the evidence is correlational
- Treating karyotype as static within a genus when intraspecific polymorphism is known for that group
- Citing chromosome counts from pre-1970 literature without noting potential taxonomic or methodological issues
- Confusing 2n (diploid number) with n (haploid number) in quantitative claims
